Abstract

We are born into a society with very defined rules and traditions, which we accept and follow without a question. In Western society, the Industrial Revolution, Descartes’philosophical views and the power of Christianity on society have made a big impact in the way we connect and view nature nowadays. We went from being part of nature to putting it as the object we observe, distancing ourselves from it more and more. We own everything in it and do what we want with it. We have made sure to prove our power over our surrounding and non-humans. Leading to the current Climate Crisis, we are causing our own suffering and we need to learn how to stop the pain. We need to finally understand that we are part of nature, and by understanding that we might be able to save ourselves.

Introduction

Sometimes, I find myself spending hours that seem like minutes looking out of my window. Luckily, my window faces a clear empty sky and a canal accompanied by big leafy trees, which immediately make me more relaxed. I have to think how wonderful the things are, we take for granted.. And this thesis is about this, about what we take for granted. It is about nature, about our Mother Nature, Gaia in Greek mythology, the ancestral mother of all life, that I will write. The mother that we keep on damaging, burning, contaminating, destroying and abusing. Our home. The womb in which we were created.

Why do we harm her? Who gave us humans the right to do as we please? Why do we abuse when we could value instead, treasure? Many Western religions are based on the anthropocentric viewpoint, which argues that human beings are the central or most significant entities in the world. Can we humans escape from this human–centered worldview?

In this thesis I want to explore the relationship between humans and nature, including animals. More precisely, I want to answer the previous questions and research why and how we have learnt what we consider culture and the origins of our society’s anthropocentric view, by exploring different fields like philosophy, psychology, history, and religion.

The motivation for this topic is to burst the bubble we are used living in, and help others and myself understand our surrounding with more clarity. Furthermore, I want this thesis to be a bit of an eye-opener that will lead us to a more empathetic and conscious society in which we are careful and thoughtful in the actions that we decide to make.

Lastly, I want to go through an introspective trip to the past, analyzing what could have influenced me in finding this topic so relevant and find out what were the events that had such an impact in the way society became what it is nowadays and its relationship with its surrounding.

My Nature

Beginnings, context and first realizations

"Oh, this girl is going to have a hard time in life". This is what my mum thought after coming to the rescue when she heard me crying my eyes out after I had killed an ant while playing in our bathroom when I was around 7 years old. I remember killing it as a reflex, probably because I had seen others do that too, or maybe not. Then, immediately after, I started crying and thinking: “The ant was there, doing its thing not harming anyone, why did I have to kill it?” This and similar situations, added to my uncontrollable childhood hobby of saving objects that had broken at home from being trashed, as if they were trash. I couldn’t stop putting myself in the place of animals, objects, and nature, telling myself: “I wouldn’t want that to happen to me either.”
I always guessed it was just a personality trait, no one could tell me more than “You’re just a hypersensitive person, and that’s ok”, and so I was kind of content with that. But the older I got the more I was thinking: “But, why? Why do those things affect me so much more than to others?”

In 2017, I came across a very interesting talk by James Wildman, a humane educator at the Animal Rights Foundation of Florida (ARFF). In this video, he tells the audience that the circles shown in the screen, a red and a blue one, are not the same size, and asks to raise their hands to the ones that think the red one is bigger than the blue one, and the other way around. With this innocent game, he makes the audience see that as well as in society, all that we have learnt, we have accepted and never questioned (1). Even though his case was about animal rights and how we eat, it can also be applied to many other cases. The key point here is to ask oneself: Why do I do what I do? Is it something I decided myself, or have I automatically absorbed it from society?

As kids, we learn what our parents teach us and we believe that is the truth. The same happens to individuals in this family to which we belong, called society. We take what is given and without questioning it, we follow. But there’s a moment in life, for some, when we realize those things we thought were true or right, aren’t. We grow up and build our own personality. As a toddler I was exposed, as many other children, to cartoons from the cartoon conglomerate Walt Disney Pictures, from its cartoons characters such as Mickey Mouse, Donald Duck and Goofy to its films The Lion King, The Beauty and the Beast, and Alice in Wonderland, to name a few. In all of these films we see animals, flowers and/or objects interacting as if they were human: with a voice, a personality, emotions, and human facial expressions. The decision of humanizing, or better said, anthropomorphizing1, was made with the intention of making the human viewer connect and understand the (hidden) message in a clearer way. That is, the more human we perceive the animal to be, the easier it is for us to relate to it and develop empathy. This can lead to some of the most absurd situations (2).

Situations in which animal cartoons are anthropomorphized to a point that they become a hybrid between animal and human, animals behaving like humans forgetting they are animals themselves. This doesn’t happen only in Disney movies. Other examples that had a great impact on the distorted idea I had about non-humans and objects are cartoons such as The Looney Tunes and movies like Dr Dolittle. The last one is about the stories of a doctor and surgeon that can talk with animals in their own language and helps them treating them as his patients. In this movie, animals are given human voices, emotions, and gestures (3).

This can have negative effects on the viewer such as not being able to see the difference between animals and humans. It is no surprise I wished wholeheartedly, that my dog would start laughing and ask me to stop when I tickled her.

This could be seen as a positive thing, since the anthropomorphization of all those characters would make me more respectful and empathetic. But is this really the right way to do it? It might not even be a surprise if I said that, at one point in my life, we owned six different animals: a hamster, a rabbit, a dog, a ferret, a turtle, and two parakeets. Our house, that looked like a mad house filled with animals, was so dreamy to me; I liked animals so much that I wanted more and more… I didn’t ever ask myself if we had too many, my love for them was blinding me. We gradually stopped having so many pets but the memory of it remained like a dream. Until one day, when I visited a zoo again after not having stepped in one for over ten years. There was something different. Did the zoo change or did I change? Did I change or did my perception change? Suddenly, the place I remember enjoying so much as a child looked so depressing, soul-less, and dark. I wanted to leave that place as fast as possible. Ever since, I felt guilt and shame and can’t stop wondering: Why do we do the things we do to non–humans?

Yesterday's Nature

How the society I grew up in has been shaped

But first, what is nature? Nature is the natural, physical, or material world or universe. It can refer to the phenomena of the physical world, and also to life in general. All that is part of the Earth.
In Greek mythology, Gaea represents the Earth. Gaea is life. She is all, the very soul of the earth and, for all intents and purposes, the Mother of Everything Beautiful in the world. Born spontaneously – either out of Nothingness or out of Chaos, she is the ancestral mother of all life.

Even more, the term “Mother Earth” is recognized by the UN General Assembly as a common expression used to refer to the planet in various countries and regions. The term demonstrates the unavoidable interdependence between human beings, other living species and the globe. Although humans are part of nature, human activity is often understood as a separate category from other natural phenomena.

This separation is the result of an event that marked a major turning point in society: The Industrial Revolution, which consisted in the process of change from an agrarian and handicraft economy to one dominated by industry and machine manufacturing in Europe and the United States, in the period from about 1760 to sometime between 1820 and 1840. The creation of factories in cities and the migration of the majority of the population from the countryside to the cities caused a major distancing from nature. In 1800, only 3% of the world's population lived in cities, while, nowadays, it’s nearly 50%. Formerly, humans had been part of nature; then they became the exploiters of nature. This didn’t only affect our daily lives in the sense that we moved from one location to another - leaving the countryside for the urban area -, but also our very perception of what we left behind.
Cities started growing at a very fast pace, the countryside surrounding became habitable area and field animals were rarely seen. During the first part of the Industrial Revolution, animals were used as machines, a phase in which the view of the philosopher René Descartes, which believed that animals were unfeeling machines, was confirmed (we will talk more in detail about Descartes later on). Eventually, the internal combustion engine replaced animals and once they were removed from streets and factories, they were treated as plain raw material. Animals moved from being considered a tool, to being required for food and processed like a manufactured commodity.
Nowadays, when we think about nature, we visualize hills, trees, and flower fields (4).

We owe the idealized concept of nature to Romanticism: an artistic, literary, musical and intellectual movement originated in Europe towards the end of the 18th century. It was characterized by its emphasis on emotion and individualism as well as glorification of all the past and nature, preferring the medieval rather than the classical. The idealization of nature was partly caused by the Industrial Revolution, which basically removed all signs of nature from the daily life replacing it with factories. Romantics wanted to get rid of the separation between subject and object, between man and the natural world. Furthermore, they were distrustful of the human world and believed having a close connection with nature was mentally and morally healthy.

However, not only the urbanization of societies shaped the society in which we live now. A society is also shaped by two very important elements that dictate the belief and thought of this society: religion and philosophy.

Religion

As the historian Lynn White says in his essay The Historical Roots of our Ecological Crisis (1967), "What people do about their ecology depends on what they think about themselves in relation to things around them."" Religious views have a strong influence on the perspective that may allow people to ignore the problems or may force them to confront the issues. That perspective on the world is often unnoticed or omitted and it can differ completely from one religion to another, even from one religious practitioner to another.
Being born in Europe means, that Christianity will be the religion that will rule your life in a way or another, either you are a practicing or not. It is true that the way we think and talk are no longer considered Christian, but we still find some traces of it into our culture. As a simple example, we might say we are atheists and still celebrate Christmas (or Feast of the Nativity). Originally, Christmas was meant to commemorate the birth of Jesus Christ, although nowadays has been turned into the festive period of the year, which promotes consumerism more than anything else. But that’s another topic. Going back to Christianity, what did it tell people about their relationship with nature, their environment?

Christianity got from Judaism a story of creation of the world and is as follows: God created light and darkness, then he created the Earth and its plants, animals, birds, and fishes. Finally he created Adam and then Eve to keep him from being lonely. Man named all animals confirming his superiority over them. Man is not part of nature because he is made in God’s image.

White states that: “Especially in its Western form, Christianity is the most anthropocentric religion the world has seen. […] not only establishing a dualism of man and nature but also insisting that it is God's will that man exploit nature for his proper ends.” The following verse of the Bible strikingly illustrates this point:



Genesis 1:26
And God said, Let us make man in our image, after our likeness: and let them have dominion over the fish of the sea, and over the fowl of the air, and over the cattle, and over all the earth, and over every creeping thing that creepeth upon the earth



In antiquity, every tree, every mountain, every river, every rock had its guardian spirit. This was pagan animism (animism being used as a term for the belief system of many indigenous people), the belief that places, objects and creatures possess a spiritual essence, that a soul also exists in them and not only in humans. It was important that the spirit of a particular case was appeased before a mountain was mined or before a tree was cut, and so on. With Christianity, the spirits in natural objects that protected nature from man vanished.
Going back to the question of “What did [Christianity] tell people about their relationship with nature, their environment?” We might find answers when reading the Bible and, although this is one way of interpreting the holy book, led mostly by Lynn White’s work, I find it especially interesting and strong. Although the Bible isn’t the justification for environmental abuse, it still had a strong influence in the way our surrounding has been and is perceived.

Philosophy

Do we humans fear nature? Have we always feared nature? Have we been so aware of its power that we needed to tame it and control it? According to the art critic, novelist, painter and poet John Berger, “The life of a wild animal becomes an ideal, an ideal internalized as a feeling surrounding a repressed desire.” To me, what Berger comes to say is that we idealize the way non-animals live and we wished we could live like them, and that’s our repressed desire.
Philosophy is concerned with the question of how an individual lives, could and should live, and therefore, strongly influences a society. Coccia, in his book The Life of Plants, criticizes the philosophy we know and says: “Philosophy seeks at all costs to be human and humanistic, to be included among the human and social sciences like all others. (…) Philosophy has turned into a sort of Don Quixote of contemporary knowledge, engaged in an imaginary struggle against the projections of its own spirit; on into a Narcissus who looks back at the ghosts of its past, now empty souvenirs in a provincial museum.”3 With this, Coccia argues that philosophy has focused on an anthropocentric viewpoint, seeing human beings as the central or most significant entities in the world. Also, this philosophy considers that humans are superior and completely separate from nature and states that human life is especially valuable while other entities (including animals, plants, mineral resources, and so on) are simply resources that can be exploited for the benefit of humankind. This is a basic belief embedded in many Western religions and philosophies. René Descartes, the father of modern Western philosophy, held that only humans are conscious. Only humans have minds and souls, can learn, only humans have language. Therefore only humans are deserving of compassion. According to Descartes, animals have only a body and not a soul. This distinguishes humans from animals, and places humans at the center of the world. Descartes maintained that animals cannot reason and do not feel pain; that animals might be living organic creatures, but automata, like mechanical robots. Unfortunately for animals, [Western] science adopted his view.4
In opposition, the philosopher David Schweitzer rejected this view. Schweitzer was a pioneer in the world of animal protection and strongly believed that “Until we extend our circle of compassion to all living things, humanity will not find peace.” All living creatures are considered as one: “I am life that wills to live in the midst of life that wills to live”.

Although other philosophers like Schweitzer were opposed to the dualism by Descartes, this one had a strong effect on the society of the time. It might have been the very beginning of our modern society: The human being as the protagonist of their own story, completely separated from animals, declaring the superiority of humans over other species.

Today's Nature

The current Human-Nature relationship

Going back to what I have been wondering for many years: Why do we do the things we do to non–humans? I venture to say that one of human beings’ current biggest fears might be the future of their existence under the climate crisis. Trying to find solutions to prolong the human existence. It is well known that the climate crisis has been caused by human activity: temperatures rising, sea level increasing, forests drying out or burning down, animal species going extinct. Now the damage is done and we do have to find solutions. To find solutions we must get back to my first question: how did we get here? And why? Our human history of making other species suffer has led to a realistic near future scenario, where it is our turn to do the suffering.
Dr. Kahn, director of the Human Interaction With Nature and Technological Systems (HINTS) Lab in Washington, answers this question with a theory that he named generational amnesia, which means that in every generation the amount of environmental degradation can increase but each generation finds that degradation as non-degraded. Each generation perceives their environmental impact as natural, because it can’t be compared with anything else. A comparative experiential baseline is missed. As an example I could talk about a field I used to visit together with my family as a child. It was a wild and leafy field in which we would let our dog run freely for hours. Every time we visited, the field was less wild and less leafy… it turned out that there was a plan of turning that field into a golf course. Around 12 years later we went back to that spot with my 10-year-old cousin who never got the chance to meet that wild and leafy field. For her, the golf course was normal, it was just there and it was ok, she couldn’t compare the before with the after, she couldn’t see what actually had had to be destroyed in order for that golf course to be there. And this is problematic because people then don’t recognize that there is a problem and if we can’t recognize that, we cannot solve that problem.

Is it possible to reconcile humans and nature? A first step to get closer to this connection could be evaluating if we would accept that the things we do to others would be done to us the same way. Here comes the memorable quote by the Chinese philosopher Confucius: “Don't do unto others what you don't want done unto you.”
With his book Topsy Turvy World, the Berlin based artist Georg Barber, also known as Atak, depicts images of rabbits shooting hunters, lions keeping humans and more, subverting images that have become everyday in order to show their grotesqueness and perversion (6).


While the images might be seen as silly or simply funny to some, to others, it is a moment of realization and beginning of de-centralization of the human being in the anthropocentric society we live in. How many of us have confidently said how much we love animals and how much we love nature? Many of us love our pet dog/cat, or admired the great elegance of a horse, or have desperately needed a walk in the forest to feel better or simply to escape the urban life for a while.
Since the beginning of our species we had a daily and intimate connection with the natural world. That need to connect - deeply and intimately - with nature is still with us; it's part of our human nature. This is known as biophilia, which translates to “love of life”, a term first used by the humanistic philosopher and social psychologist Erich Fromm to describe the psychological orientation of being attracted to everything that is alive and vital. Humans are innately attracted to nature and we can find as an example the symbolic use of nature in our language. We regularly use idioms such as blind as a bat, to horse around, fishy, among others, which are clear evidences of biophilia. “Animals lent their name or character to a quality, which like all qualities was mysterious”5. Such affiliations with natural metaphors seem to come from times when people had a much closer contact with nature than most do today. Biophilia is not only limited to the tendency of affiliating with nature but it also extends to the beneficial effects that we get when looking at it.
We, humans, take the position of the subject and put nature as the object we look at, always taking that distance by observing it as something separate from us. Following this same concept, Berger expresses that also animals are always the objects we observe and the fact they can observe us is not important, all we know about them is proof of our power which is what separates us from them6. It’s thought that human separation from the natural world occurred together with the development of technology, changing human interactions with nature. The dependence of the human species on technology has reduced the human desire to connect with nature, or also called, biophilic behavior. The biologist Edward O.Wilson and others have argued that this decline could cause the loss of human respect for the natural world. In fact, the loss of desire to interact with nature and the little appreciation of life forms that support human survival are potential factors that contribute to destruction of the environment and the fast pace in which species become extinct.

I’ve always been fascinated about the contradictory behavior of human beings towards nature. We exploit it, chop it, burn it, contaminate it, but at the same time we desire it. Why do humans tend to destroy what we love? We might not do those actions as individuals but those are clearly and systematically done by our species. Especially in cities, we ignore the real nature but seek a fake one and we introduce it into our houses, into our daily lives. The representation of nature on different surfaces creates a living illusion.
My fellow student Kexin Hao explores this phenomenon on her Instagram account portrayed_landscapes in which she publicly posts imagery of depicted nature blended in our daily lives. “The more we portray nature, the more distant and opposed we are from it.”
We need to see nature in our daily lives but ignore to really feel it. “There are so many urban objects that are decorated with landscape prints and wraps, everywhere I go, in every city and every corner of the world. They suggest that the nature “WOULD better be there” but “ IS not there”, this contrast of ideals and reality, and the absence of realness is embarrassing.”

She continues with “the landscape is portrayed, fantasied, longed for and impulsed, after the landscape is expelled from the urban space. Then, she ends with “I can understand [now] how much people urge to have nature in urban spaces, how little they care if it is real or not, and how little they understand nature” (7).

These portrayed landscape in our daily lives might have made us even more distant than what we would have expected in the first place. Similar to this is the tendency to collect sunsets on our phones, a trend that came along the launch of cellphones with cameras. The constant need of collecting those moments in our phones disconnect us from reality. What happens when we substitute the exploration of reality to that of technological reality? What happens when we start exploring the outside world from the inside? “As we lose the richness and depth of pervasiveness of interaction with nature, we shift the baseline of what is recognized as healthy nature experience.” Says Dr Kahn, author of Technological Nature: Adaptation and the Future of Human Life. But does that only happen with nature? Among the so many trends that society has come up with, some have achieved the realm of the absurd. To start with one we could start with the trend of owning a very specific dog breed. This considerably new trend, starting probably with Paris Hilton carrying her Chihuahua everywhere in her purse in the early 2000s, has caused a remarkable disconnection between human and other (domestic) animals (8).

In the article 'Fashion accessory' dogs being dumped in record numbers at animal shelter from 2011, Lara Alford, manager from The Blue Cross, a rehoming center for pets in the UK, states that 'some people bought [dogs] as fashion accessories after celebrities like Paris Hilton made them trendy. Although people think they look great when they see someone famous with one, they often don't realize what a responsibility they are taking on.”
In the past, domestic animals were kept because of a useful purpose: guard dogs would protect the house, cats to hunt mice, among others. Nowadays, the family unit in which the animals find themselves lacks earth, other animals, natural temperatures, and so on. The pet is often sterilized, with limited contact with other animals, with little chance to exercise as much as needed, and is given artificial food. This may be the thought behind the expression that pets come to resemble their masters or mistresses 9 (9).

There is a tension between the roles of animals as companions to humans, and their existence as individuals with rights of their own. Humanizing animals makes them lose their identity;they feel frustrated,anxious, and insecure. It is morally reprehensible because it imposes unnecessary suffering on the animal. The owners impose disrespectful human rules that go from hairstyles to clothing, and that goes against the identity of the animal. This tendency of collecting has gone beyond the standard pets: nowadays, pets can be cats, dogs, birds, hamsters, fish, and so on. Our desire to own animals unusual as pets often leads us to bring wild animals home. As an example, a few years back, YouTube videos went viral because of the super cute creature in them, a pet slow loris. This animal, which is native to Southeast Asia, became highly demanded because of the popularity of those videos. This kind of intense attachment towards other animals can lead to illegal trade of wild animals. In addition to that, there have been cases of a pet chimp attacking its owner or a big cat hurting its keeper and they are not rare, what is rare is that we are still surprised by animals behaving like animals (10).

This leads us back to the question of why anthropomorphism is a bad thing. While the act of giving human attributes to animals, nature and objects can help us in connecting and emphasizing with them in a way that could lead to their preservation, we are still positioning ourselves as the center. That is, we are only accepting the behavior of non-human creatures when it resembles ours. Can’t we not feel empathy and respect just by letting them be their own selves? Furthermore, anthropomorphism is often limited to social, intelligent animals, to which we can relate more, such as chimpanzees, pandas, polar bears and dolphins – often at the expense of species we find less cute or attractive or that we find we don’t connect much with in any way, such as chickens, insects, or fish. This leads to speciesism, which is the differing treatment or moral consideration of individuals based on the species they belong to. One of the most common comparisons is that of the cow and the dog; the cow is bred and killed for food while dogs are treated as companion animals.

Why do we anthropomorphize the non-anthropomorphic? Even if it is to seek connection with non-human creatures, we must question our motivations. For example, are we acting merely out of self-interest of being less lonely, or are we fully respecting the autonomy of the creature we seek to connect with?
Yet this kind of toxic relationship and the tendency of owning nature don’t only apply to animals. One of the most common trends in the last years has been houseplants. Because nearly half of the world's population lives in urban areas and mostly in small spaces, our connection with nature has been reduced. Although we might have a few parks around, we are not in touch with real nature anymore. The easiest way to feel closer to nature on a daily basis is by having houseplants in your home (11).

The trend has extended to all sorts of plants, including tropical plants in countries where its weather is everything but tropical but for some reason (probably caused by climate change) they survive in areas where they were not meant to exist. The different temperatures we exposed them to and the polluted urban air, the lack of real light and the excess of artificial light, the chemical products we spray them with, the lack of space, of proper care or patience to cure a plant…how many times have I rescued a plant from the street that just needed some time, some care? Thrown out in the street like a broken and unfixable object that can be easily replaced by a new one. While owning exotic plants might make us feel original, this shows the way actually perceive nature. Don’t we also pick wild flowers to make our own decorative flower bouquets? Both plants and flowers become part of our home-décor, another piece of furniture or a simple object. With the success of Instagram, more and more people are open to share their interiors, which rarely lack at least one big plant. The more plants, the better, but, is owning plants not part of the same tradition of appropriating nature, as owning animals is? Plants owners tend to treat their plants as their babies, making them depend completely on their owners for water and sunlight. Is there a big difference between a tropical plant in a pot in our living room and a fish in an aquarium?
The transformation of a plant into a decorative object has extended to the design field. The urge to incorporate nature into our lives seems so big; we have created the most curious objects and spaces. From houses incorporated in a mountain – as architect Alberto Ponis does - to growing trees into objects such as chairs, lampshades, and mirror frames. The latter is a project by furniture designer Gavin Munro and although it does sound like a good way to reduce wood waste, in the process, trees are trained along pre-defined routes following a plastic mold and manipulated to create the exact shape of the desired object. While these seem ideas that could lead us to a more respectful/environmental friendly way of living, we are still on the way of the natural course of, in this case, a tree or even a mountain.

Here I find myself again with the same, yet somewhat reformulated question: Why is it that human desires seem so persistent and dominant, we want them to be met at all cost?
Opposite to the previous design ideas, there is the technique called biomimicry, or biomimetics, which aims to solve human problems emulating elements of nature. Us humans have looked at nature for answers to problems throughout our existence. Nature has taught us things and we could solve a lot of (engineering) problems thanks to it. Problems like environmental exposure tolerance, self-healing abilities and hydrophobicity (also known as impermeability). Another example I came across with a long time ago was a video of a tailorbird sewing its own nest with plant fiber as the thread. While watching at the precise work of the tailorbird I couldn’t stop thinking about the mental switch that just had happened in my head: We don’t know everything and what we know, is only thanks to nature.

Tomorrow's Nature

Understanding of the Natural World


It’s not impossible to imagine a respectful society in which humans coexist with nature. Everything we need is given to us, and the way to be thankful is by respecting it. Therefore, the leading question of this fourth and final chapter is: Can humans reconnect with nature?
The pandemic we experienced in early 2020 has been affecting us in many ways and remains in our memory as a traumatic experience, regardless of individual experience. What is certain is, during the lockdown period, the longing for nature increased considerably. Is it true then, that we don’t appreciate what we have until we actually loose it? Or, slightly different, that the inaccessibility was the main cause of its desire? As previously mentioned, our tendency to connect with our environment is part of our human nature. Many people that could be considered as conformists or maybe even deniers of what is happening or can be done to avoid what is actually happening, might be familiarized with the phrase “We will adapt”, meaning that whatever happens to the environment, the nature or the planet, we’ll adapt, we’ll be Ok. Dr Kahn criticizes that adaptation is not singular; it’s not just one change what we have to do. There are different types of adaptation such as “genetic change, homeostasis, acclimatization, addiction, habituation, equilibration, assimilation, and accommodation.” Only by being able to go through all those types of adaptation we will be able to be Ok.
“We will adapt” seems to be a fruitful starting point, when talking about the biggest natural crisis we’ve ever experienced: that of climate change.

Climate Crisis

The Climate Crisis is nothing new, we all have seen, read and heard what are its consequences: rise of sea level, drought, floods, rise of temperatures, and so on. There are studies and information within reach that everyone has access to. Yet for some reason not much is done about it. The philosopher Slavoj Zizek illustrates this problem by pointing at our belief that garbage, that it vanishes, disappears… But garbage just moves from one place to another, always being part of the world. “I feel that man, living in our region, is simply suffocating in his own life among the garbage since there is nowhere to take it, nowhere to sweep it out – we have lost the border between garbage space and non-garbage space. Everything is covered up, littered with garbage – our homes, streets, cities”.
When it comes to love, Zizek states that if we love the world in which we live, we shall love it with all its imperfections, in this case the imperfections he refers to is the garbage. Real love does not idealise the other, one also has to love the imperfections of the other, so we should love the world including all the garbage in it. This idea is interesting when we think about the present, about our surroundings and all the garbage that has already been produced. However, making peace with the current roaming garbage may not exactly redeem us from our actions: in fact, making peace with garbage may actually pave the way for a continuance of growing waste. Yet without the guilty feeling attached to it.

In his documentary Life on Earth, the natural historian David Attenborough emphasizes on the collective behavior of humans throughout history and all its striking consequences. The decline of our planet’s resources is rather new, only in the last hundred years the change has been drastic. We consume the Earth until its destruction, nothing can stop us. Some habitats are disappearing, no ecosystem is safe, not even the seas. Half of fertile soil of the Earth is farmland and we turned wild animals into domestic animals. The 70% of birds are domestic birds, mostly chicken, we humans represent one third of the weight of all mammals, the other 60% are animals that we breed for food, the rest of the animals represent only 4% (from mice to whales). This is our world, a world ruled by us and for us. There’s no much left for the others, the non-human world has disappeared. We’ve infested the world.
As possible solutions, Attenborough proposes that we should replant the Earth, restore biodiversity, very easy actions that could benefit us all. The way to do it is to slow down or cease natality, put simply, to have less children. On top of that, changing our diet would be the biggest and most effective change. The planet Earth cannot bear thousands of millions of carnivores, there is not enough room. For each predator there are more than hundred prey, every time we eat a piece of meat we also demand a large amount of space. If we all had a vegetarian diet, we would need less of the ground we have now. Forests are the best technology to block carbon and are the center of biodiversity. The more dense they are, the more effective to catch the carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. Only by accepting this reality can we do something about it. It’s about time that our species cease to increase.

Once I came across a quote from an unknown source that said: “It's not the deer that crosses the road, it's the road that crosses the forest". Although this quote might leave some unimpressed, it definitely made an impact on me. To me, it is about switching the position in which we’re used to see things, the way we observe the world around us. It’s that moment of actually questioning what came first. Coccia states that “plants are the real mediators: they are the first eyes that appeared and opened themselves onto the world, they are the gaze that came to perceive it in all its forms. The world is, above all, everything the plants could make of it. They are the ones who made our world, even though the status of this making is quite different from that of any other activity of living beings.”

A few months ago I made an online questionnaire and asked several people from my circle of (Facebook) friends if they felt positive about the future considering the Climate Crisis we’re currently experiencing. The 85,96% of people answered no. This left only a 14,04% of people that still had some hope. When asked how did they visualize the Earth in the far future, many responses had things in common: grey, dirty, dry in some areas and flooded in others, plastic everywhere, impersonal. While, when asked how did they visualize a dreamy eco-friendly planet, the responses varied but many coincided that in a utopian world there would be less humans, less meat, more trees, more animals and blue skies.
There are two different positions that humans can take in relation to environmental issues. One is the position of knowing that we are all threatened but not believing it entirely because the thought of the world ending is unimaginable, because we are not wired to imagine something like that, and we are not ready to do anything radical to change our way of living either. Opposite to this position is the other one which is knowing the threat, knowing that nothing can actually be done to influence the process which may lead to one’s ruin but it is too difficult to not do anything about it even though whatever is done might be completely meaningless. The latter would bring us to Green Capitalism, which is the view that sees natural resources as capital and its profits should be used for the protection and sustainability of the environment.

Many companies had to rethink their marketing strategy towards environmentally conscious customers by marketing as green products such as organic food, less toxic house cleaners, green cars and such but “to save the humans, what we need is not green cars, not electric cars, but massively fewer cars.” After all the green initiatives, the ecological contradiction of capitalism still remains: maximizing profit and saving the planet are completely opposite and cannot be aligned. Green capitalism is not a solution. It’s a delusion. There is no capitalist solution to our ecological crisis. We either save our planet or capitalism,but not both at the same time.14

Our society has been very active in the last years trying to find ways to produce less waste in an individual level and some even make drastic changes in the way they live by living the most natural life possible. However, thinking we can connect with nature by leaving our materialistic life behind and making use exclusively of what the Earth offers us, is nothing new. Living an eco-friendly life has become more popular in our western societies and something that we should take into account is that some of the practices that we came up with in order to live a greener life might have come from indigenous people. This phenomena is called “whitewashed hope”. We should be careful to not take certain land practices from Indigenous cultures without knowing their context. Instead, we may pursue the embrace of deeper Indigenous worldviews... Inspiring a consciousness shift that will support us to go from a dominant culture of supremacy and domination to one founded on reciprocity, respect, and interrelations with all beings—including, of course, among all humans. Are we capable of respecting nature while also respecting other cultures? “Indigenous people make up only 6.2% of our global population, they steward 80% of Earth’s biodiversity while managing over 25% of her land. Indigenous worldviews are the bedrocks that our agricultural practices and lifeways arise from.” Indigenous people are deeply connected with nature; the two are equal and depend on each other, nature is highly respected and they have a strong sense of place and belonging. How can we, western people, make a consciousness shift, without appropriating traditions from other cultures?
Nature will find its way to survive as it always does, we find several examples in areas that, for different reasons, have been abandoned and in which nature has found its way to take over. Following this thinking, Emanuele Coccia believes so as well when he says “Plants never cease to develop and grow, to construct new organs and new parts of their own body. Their body is a morphogenetic industry that knows no interruption”.

The Climate Crisis, then, becomes a personal issue, a human issue. We are not just damaging the Earth but we’re causing our own extinction. Only by making the problem personal, we understand the seriousness of the matter, because, again, of our anthropocentric view of the world. When we realize that Climate Change is about the survival of the human race we might be able to do something about it. Once we decide to do something about it, such as changing our diet, we also have to be aware of not “borrowing” indigenous practices that can’t be adapted to the way we live. We must understand once and for all that we do not rule the world, that there are ways to save ourselves without appropriating anything from other cultures and that we all belong to the same space and we are all connected. Why wouldn’t we lower down our imperialist behavior for the benefit of the entire world, for a change?

Conclusion

By doing an intense research on the history of how our society has disconnected itself from nature and my observation of the current state of it, I was able to answer many of the questions that have been roaming in my head for many years now. When observing different situations in which humans were mistreating and damaging animals and their surrounding I couldn’t stop wondering what led us to that behavior: Why do we treat nature as a mere decoration that can be moved or removed? Why do we objectify it? Why do we collect animals?
I learned what were the main things that influenced our society to what it is nowadays and understood that only by knowing the past, we can create a better future. It was shocking to see and accept that I’m part of this whole society and, despite of my good intentions, I (indirectly) contributed to this Western behavior towards our surrounding. This research made me more aware of the fact that we were and still are part of nature although we have forgotten about it, also to be even more empathetic towards it and to be more careful about how my actions can affect others. At first I was scared of the things I was going to find but the more I found and the more I read, the better I wanted to become as a human.
It was challenging to deal with such a big topic and I’m aware I haven’t talked about many things but this research is not ending here; this is just the beginning. I aim to continue evolving to build a deeper connection with the space I inhabit and the creatures that coexist in it.
